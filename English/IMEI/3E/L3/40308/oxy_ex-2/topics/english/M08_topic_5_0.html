4.0.0 Temporary Grounding Terms

The U.S. Department of the Interior, Bureau of Reclamation, in Denver, Colorado has listed the following terms that are associated with personal protective (temporary) grounding:

  • Exposure voltage – A short-duration difference in potential between conductive objects that a person may contact when personal protective grounds or a grounding system conduct fault current. Also applicable to transferred potential between separately grounded systems (stations), or difference in earth surface potentials.

  • Grounding (ground) – The connection of conductive parts of lines, structures, and equipment to earth or other conductive medium (grounding system) that substitutes for earth, e.g. station ground mat conductors.

  • Grounded work site – A work area that is made an equipotential safe working zone by the application of personal protective grounds. An equipotential safe working zone is one in which all surface areas in the zone are electrically the same so that no one gets shocked, and the equipment is not damaged by stray voltages. If a work site is properly grounded, there should be no step-voltage or touch-voltage issues.

  • Live-dead-live test – A test to ensure that work to be performed is being performed on a de-energized circuit. For circuits energized at 50 volts or greater, the technician making the test shall first use a known energized power source to verify that the testing device is functioning properly. This is the first live part of the test. After the operation of the test device is verified, the technician can move inside the minimum approach distance of the circuit being tested to perform a voltage check, for the purpose of verifying that a circuit is de-energized (dead). After testing the de-energized circuit, the technician returns to the known energized source and retests the testing device to verify that it is still functional (second live). These steps are the live-dead-live test. In the event that a work location has no known power source on which to check the testing device, two independent testing devices, which have been tested on a known live circuit and verified to operate properly prior to use, shall be utilized to verify that the targeted circuit is in fact de-energized.

  • Static ground – Any grounding cable or bonding jumper (including clamps) that has an ampacity (current rating) less than the maximum available fault current at the work site, or is smaller than #2 AWG (American Wire Gauge) copper equivalent. Static grounds are used for potential equalizing between conductive parts in grounding configurations that cannot subject them to significant current. Therefore, a smaller wire, which provides adequate mechanical strength, is sufficient (for example, #12 AWG).

  • Station – For protective grounding purposes, any electrical facility with a grounding electrode system (ground mat), which bonds all conductive, noncurrent-carrying parts of equipment, and for the control of surface potential gradients (changes in temperature or pressure over a given distance). Two or more distinct but adjacent facility grounding electrode systems that are intentionally bonded, such as a power plant and adjacent switchyard grounding systems, may be considered a common station grounding system. Grounding systems that are intentionally bonded, but not physically adjacent, are considered separately grounded.

  • Step voltage – The difference in surface potential experienced by a person bridging a distance of one meter, with the feet, without contacting any other grounded object.

  • Touch voltage – The difference in potential between a grounded structure or station and the surface potential at the point where a person is standing while at the same time having a hand in contact with the grounded structure or object. Figure 3 shows examples of step and touch voltages.

  • Transferred touch voltage – A special case of touch voltage where a voltage is conducted toward or away from a grounded structure or station to a remote point. A transferred touch voltage (potential) can be contacted between the hands or hands and feet.

  • Fault circuit impedance X/R ratio – Ratio of reactance to resistance of the electrical impedance of a faulted (short) circuit from the source of fault current to the location of the fault on the circuit.

  • Line terminal and equipment ground switches – Permanently installed mechanical switches, which are kept in the open position until utilized to ground line or equipment conductors during periods of maintenance.

These terms will help you understand the specific distances that you must be aware of when working on or near energized electrical equipment. OSHA Standard 1910.269(I) (10) addresses several electrical situations and provides recommended distances for various voltage levels. Figure 4 shows OSHA Standard 1910.269(I)(10) Table R-6 that shows the minimum approach distances associated with opening circuits under load.

This OSHA table recommends an approach distance of 2'-1" for voltages in the 1.1 to 15.0 kilovolt range if the exposure voltage is phase-to-ground voltage. That distance increases a little if the exposure voltage is phase-to-phase voltage. As the voltage levels increase, so do the exposure distances.

Anyone working with electrical power should already be familiar with the NEC® guidelines, but they must also be aware of state and local electrical codes, and OSHA guidelines. OSHA Standard 29 CFR 1926.954 includes the requirements for the grounding of equipment to protect personnel. NFPA Standard 70B, Recommended Practice for Electrical Equipment Maintenance, includes a chapter (Chapter 7 in the 2006 edition) that deals specifically with personnel safety and with de-energizing and grounding of electrical equipment. Because there is no way that this module could cover the details of all these guidelines, only the basics are addressed here.
