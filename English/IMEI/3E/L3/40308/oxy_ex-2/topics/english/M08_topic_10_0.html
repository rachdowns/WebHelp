Section Nine

9.0.0 Installing and Removing Temporary Grounding Devices

Some of the later paragraphs under NFPA 70B, Section 7.7.4 address the installation and removal of grounding equipment. In that text, personnel are advised to use insulated hot-line sticks, rubber gloves, or similar protective equipment while installing grounding equipment on ungrounded, de-energized overhead line conductors and also while removing the grounding equipment. For the ampacity rating of the specific cables and clamps that will be used and any application instructions, refer to data available from grounding equipment manufacturers. In some instances, specialized grounding equipment might be required, such as traveling grounds on new overhead line conductors being strung adjacent to energized circuits.

Temporary grounds are just what the name implies. They are to be used only on a temporary basis, and then removed. The removal of the grounding equipment before a circuit is intentionally re-energized is equally as important as their initial installation, but for other reasons. If grounding equipment is forgotten or overlooked after the work is completed and the circuit is intentionally re-energized, the supply circuit overcurrent protective devices will immediately open because the conductors are jumpered and grounded. The short-circuit current can damage the contacts of a breaker having adequate interrupting capacity and can cause an inadequate breaker or fuses to explode. If the grounding cables are inadequate, they can melt and initiate damaging power arcs (arc flashes).

A procedure should be established to ensure removal of all grounding equipment before the circuit is intentionally re-energized. Recommendations for such a procedure are as follows:

  1. Assign an identification number to each grounding equipment set.

  2. Make all sets available to all parties, including contractor personnel, but ensure that the sets are rigidly controlled by:

    • Maintaining a record, by number and installation location, of each set installed.

    • Ensuring that the information for each set is crossed off the record as each set is removed.

  3. Before the circuit is re-energized, ensure that all sets of grounding equipment are accounted for by number to ensure that all have been removed.

  4. The ground being used for temporary grounding shall be accessible at all times.

  5. The number and location of TPGs installed must be in accordance with local grounding procedures. Tracking the installation and removal of all TPGs is appropriate information on lockout/ tagout forms.

  6. Do not allow doors to be closed or allow them to be replaced where a set of grounding equipment has been installed inside switchgear. If it is necessary to do so to conceal grounding equipment, install a highly visible sign on the door or cover to remind personnel that a ground is inside.

  7. Before re-energizing the circuit, inspect the interiors of equipment to verify that all grounding sets, including small ones used in testing potential transformers, relays, and so on, have been removed.

  8. Before re-energizing the circuit, use a megohmmeter on all conductors to make certain that none are grounded. If so, determine the cause and correct the situation before re-energizing the conductors.

  9. Ground all equipment before performing any work on the de-energized main output of generators or transformers. Depending upon the design of the equipment, TPGs may not be required when performing certain work on de-energized switchgear parts or transformers. Refer to appropriate company procedures and vendor manuals for specifics.

Prior to the installation of any temporary grounding devices, inspect the temporary grounding equipment for broken strands in the conductors, loose connections to the clamp terminals, and defective clamp mechanisms. Also, check the test date on the equipment to make sure that it has been tested within the past 2 years. Identify defective equipment by attaching out-of-service tags. Remove defective equipment from service immediately. Some companies require that defective equipment be destroyed rather than repaired.

Grounding equipment should be installed at each point where work is being performed on de-energized equipment. Often it is advisable to install grounding equipment on each side of a work point or at each end of a de-energized circuit.
