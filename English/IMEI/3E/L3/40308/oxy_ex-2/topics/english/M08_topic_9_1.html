8.1.0 Ground Cable Assemblies

Ground cable assemblies (clamps, ferrules, and cables) must meet the material and electrical specifications of ASTM F855-04 Standard for Temporary Protective Grounds. They must also have a current rating greater than, or equal to, that of No. 2 AWG stranded copper, and they must have a protective jacket. A grounding cable must be long enough to reach from the grounding electrode to the point where the cable’s mechanical clamp will be attached to the electrical line or component. Cables will be a continuous length. Splices are not permitted. The clamps at each end must be in good condition and must be electrically and mechanically compatible to the cable. The whole assembly must be able to withstand the thermal and mechanical stresses imposed by fault currents, up to the rating of the component parts.

Most grounding cables are similar to welding cable made from extra flexible copper wire covered with an insulating material. Annealed copper conductors are mandatory. Aluminum wire is not allowed. Through normal use, and sometimes abuse, the flexible cables eventually wear and break. The breaks usually happen near the clamps. The clamps used with grounding cables vary. Different applications require different types of clamps. Ball-and-socket clamps or C-clamps with an eye screw or vise-grip (T-handle) are the preferred types. Installing a grounding clamp while standing beside a machine on the ground is a lot easier than installing a grounding clamp to a high-voltage line insulator while hanging 30 feet in the air from a power pole. The most difficult part of any grounding job is attaching the line-end clamp onto the equipment terminal or distribution line.

Most terminals are not easily accessible. The high-voltage lines coming into or out of substation equipment are usually not insulated, but the wiring inside enclosures is insulated right up to the lug securing the wires to the terminals. Attaching a clamp-on style of clamp to the insulated wires can be a challenge. The ball-and-socket clamp assemblies (Figure 10) are easier to work with when checking lugs, busbars, or terminal points, because the socket is easily slipped onto the ball. Clamps such as C-clamps are much more difficult to install when working in close quarters.

The ball part of these clamps is installed on the equipment before the equipment is ever energized. The socket part is attached to one end of the grounding cable. A hot stick is used to position the socket head so that it can be slipped over the installed ball. The installation of temporary grounding cables equipped with socket devices is considered to be energized work and must be done while using live-line tools and techniques. The distance from the installer to the electrical device usually determines the type of insulated installation device being used. After the socket head is attached, the clamping screw on the socket head is tightened to secure the socket head to the fixture.

Figure 11 shows ball-and-socket grounding clamps installed on high-voltage equipment.

The problem with installing devices such as the mounted ball onto the energized equipment is that the devices corrode. Corrosion interferes with the metal-to-metal contact needed for good grounding. When installing such grounding devices, always be sure that corrosion is not going to prevent a good grounding connection. Special devices, such as wire brushes, can be attached to the end of a hot stick to clean the on-equipment terminals. Twisting a clamp back and forth or side to side before securing it to a terminal will also help to clean the terminal.
