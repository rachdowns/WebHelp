Section Three

3.0.0 Purpose of Temporary Grounding

An electrical circuit consists of a power source tied through a designated path to a load that is grounded. As long as the electrons travel this designated or intended path, all is well. A ground fault is a situation in which electrical energy from any source finds its way through an unintended path to ground. There are various other definitions of a ground fault, but the bottom line is that if you become the unintended path, you will be injured, or even killed. Part of the reason for grounding de-energized circuits or equipment is so that work can be done on them safely.

The Occupational Safety and Health Administration (OSHA) guidelines require that employers provide their workers with a safe work environment. When electrical equipment is involved, the equipment and all inputs/outputs must be placed into an electrically safe work condition. This means that all equipment must be de-energized, discharged, and locked out before any work can be performed. After the electrical equipment has been de-energized, temporary protective grounds (TPGs) are installed to provide grounding paths to help protect anyone working on the equipment. Temporary protective grounds are also called personal protective grounding (PPG) because that is their primary purpose. They provide an alternate path to ground so that any unwanted electrical energy will follow that alternate path. Figure 2 shows a personal protective ground cable assembly with C-clamps.

Specific circumstances dictate the extent of protection that should be provided. Equipment operating at more than 50 volts should have warning labels indicating the voltage and current levels in or on the equipment. The higher the levels of voltage and current, the more dangerous the situation becomes. Anyone preparing to work on high-voltage equipment must consider these issues when placing the equipment into an electrically safe work condition. The level of electrical potential determines the kind of temporary grounding devices that should be used.

Regardless of the specific circumstances, optimum protection should be provided. NFPA 70E and NFPA 70B, Section 7.7.1 state that a high level of protection should be provided for any work on high- and medium-voltage circuits. Minimal protection might be sufficient for work on minor branch circuits. The following possible conditions and occurrences, as noted in NFPA 70B, Section 7.7.2, should be considered when determining the type and extent of protection that must be provided:

  • Induced voltage from adjacent energized conductors, which can be appreciably increased when high fault currents flow in adjacent circuits

  • Switching errors causing inadvertent re-energizing of the circuits

  • Any unusual condition that might bring an energized conductor into electrical contact with the de-energized circuit

  • Extremely high voltages caused by direct or nearby lightening strikes

  • Stored electrical charges from capacitors or other equipment.
