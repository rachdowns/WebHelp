8.6.0 Ground Tester Equipment

Visual testing of grounding cables and clamps detects only the things obvious to the human eye, but there are test equipment devices that place a load onto the grounding devices to simulate a ground fault they may have to handle. Various companies make portable testers that evaluate the resistance of PPGs. Figure 18 shows a portable grounding set tester testing a PPG.

These portable testers are powered by regular 120VAC wall outlet power. They generate a small direct current (DC) source that is used to evaluate the PPG clamps and cable. After the clamps of the PPG are attached to the two posts on the tester, the tester is turned on and started. The tester then automatically applies a low level of DC voltage, usually around 5 volts, to the PPG leads for a very short time period while it takes a reading, and then it removes the power from the PPG leads. The tester is used to make sure that the resistance of the PPG (clamps and cable) is very low. The level of resistance depends upon the size of the PPG cable and its clamps.

Testers have a calibrated test cable that is used to perform a self-check on the tester. The test cable is a known value. When placed across the two terminals of the test set, the tester is placed into a self-test mode. It provides a readout that tells the operator that the tester itself is good or bad. If the self-test readout is out of tolerance, the tester must be repaired. If the readout is good, the tester can be used to evaluate any PPG. Performing this self-test on the tester is similar to crossing the leads on an ohmmeter to zero it before taking a resistance reading across a resistor. As with an ohmmeter, you are looking for minimum resistance during this self-check test.

After a tester is evaluated, an actual PPG is attached to the tester’s two posts. The size of the PPG cable must be entered into the tester so that it will know what it is being asked to evaluate. After the PPG is connected and the tester is set, a test button or switch is activated and the tester begins the evaluation of the PPG. The tester takes into consideration the resistance of the cable, its clamps, and the terminal posts of the tester. The resistance of the cable is found by multiplying the resistance per foot by the number of feet of cable being tested. The cable clamps and connections should be less than 1 milliohm (mΩ) each. With two clamps on a PPG, their resistance would be equal to 2 mΩ. Table 1 shows the resistance (Ω) per foot value for various cable sizes.

Use the following formula to calculate the expected resistance of a 32-foot long 1/0 cable:

Example 1:

As shown in Table 1, the resistance of a 1/0 cable is 0.098 mΩ per foot. If the cable is 32 feet long, multiply 0.098 by 32, and then add that resistance to the 2 mΩ of the clamps. The result is 5.136 mΩ.

32 × 0.098 = 3.136 + 2 = 5.136

Example 2:

As shown in Table 1, the resistance of a 4/0 cable is 0.049. If this cable also is 32 feet long, multiplying 0.049 times 32 would be 1.568 mΩ of resistance for only the cable. When the 2 mΩ of the clamps is added in, the overall resistance of a PPG having a 4/0 cable is 3.568 mΩ.

32 × 0.049 = 1.568 + 2 = 3.568
