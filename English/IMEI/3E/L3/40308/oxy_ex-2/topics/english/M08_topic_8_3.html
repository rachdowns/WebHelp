7.3.0 Installation Requirements

Solid metal-to-metal connections are essential between grounding clamps and the de-energized conductors. Conductors are often corroded and are sometimes covered with paint. Ground clamps should have serrated jaws because it is often impractical to clean the conductors. The clamps should be slightly tightened in place, given a slight rotation on the conductors to provide cleaning action by the serrated jaws, and then securely tightened. Ground clamps that attach to the steel tower, switchgear, or station ground bus are equipped with pointed or cupped setscrews that should be tightened to ensure penetration through corrosion and paint, to provide adequate connections.

Grounding cables should be no longer than is necessary to keep resistance as low as possible and to minimize slack in cables to prevent their violent movement under fault conditions. If the circuit should be inadvertently re-energized, the fault current and resultant magnetic forces could cause severe and dangerous movement of slack grounding cables in the area where personnel are working. Proper routing of grounding cables to avoid excessive slack is essential for personnel safety.

Grounding cables should be connected between phases to the grounded structure and to the system neutral (when available) to minimize the voltage drop across the work area if inadvertent re-energization of the circuits should occur. Using short cables between the phases and to ground minimizes the resistance between the phases and helps reduce the reaction time of the overcurrent protective devices. Figure 8 shows the equivalent electrical diagram of grounding phases to ground.

In the electrical diagram, the person’s body (Rm) is 500 ohms. The Rj symbol is the resistance of a single cable, which can be on the order of 0.001 ohm. The Rg symbol is ground resistance of the work area. If a 1,000 ampere current was to flow in the circuit grounded in this manner, the person would be subjected to only about 1 volt imposed across the work area; therefore, the current flow through the worker’s body would be negligible. Under the conditions shown in the drawing, the same electrical situations apply to the worker on the pole, on the tower, or in the switchgear room.

Connecting the phase conductors with short cables and clamps of adequate capacity minimizes the resistance between phases. Such arrangements improve the reaction times of the overcurrent protective devices that will de-energize the circuit if it is inadvertently re-energized. The short down-lead cable between the jumpered phase conductors (Rjs) and the grounds (Rg) reduces resistance to ground. Shorter cables also reduce violent cable movements that can happen during high current flow. If there is a system neutral conductor at the work location, connect a cable to it for increased protection. This helps ensure the lowest resistance in the ground return path to the source.

In Figure 8, workers are shown inside a switchgear area, on a tower, and on a pole. If all the work areas were properly grounded as shown, anyone working in those areas would be parallel with a minimum of resistance, which means that he or she would be exposed to minimum voltage drop in the event of current flow in the system. That low resistance would cause rapid operation of the fuses or circuit breakers, thus minimizing the time the person is exposed to the voltage drop.
