Section One

1.0.0 Introduction

A valve actuator, commonly called an operator, is a device that moves the stem of a valve, either manually or automatically. The purpose of a valve actuator is to accurately position the valve’s moveable part to a desired position (opened, closed, or partially opened or closed). Manual actuators come in the form of handwheels, levers, and wrenches that are attached to the stem of the valve. Larger valves that require great effort to turn must be equipped with some type of powered mechanism, such as electric, pneumatic, or hydraulic drive units attached to the valve stem. These are referred to as automatic actuators. The entire assembly consisting of some type of motor, an actuator and a valve makes up a motor-operated valve (MOV). The operation of MOVs involves using the motor to drive an actuator that positions the valve’s movable part (the plug, ball or vane) in and out of the stationary seat of the valve.

The moveable part inside the housing of a valve moves in either a radial (rotating) motion or a lateral (side-to-side or end-to-end) motion. The moveable part has different names in different types of valves, but the part that moves the moveable part is always called the stem. Figure 1 shows the parts inside three common types of valves.

The moveable part of a globe valve is called a disc. In a gate valve, it is called a wedge. The stems in both valves are screwed into the bonnet that is attached to the body of the valves. As the stem is rotated, the screw threads of the stem and the bonnet move the stem laterally in or out, which in turn makes the disc or wedge close or open the flow path between them and their seats inside the valve body. The moveable part inside ball or butterfly valves, and others like them, rotates clockwise or counterclockwise within the valve housing. The stem in these kinds of valves is attached to the top of the rotating part and extends outside the body of the valve where a handle is attached. In a ball valve, the ball has a hollow passageway through its center. When the ball is turned as shown in Figure 1, flow is allowed to pass through the valve. When the ball is turned 90 degrees, the solid walls of the ball block the flow path. In other valves whose moveable parts rotate, their moveable parts also either allow, block, or somehow restrict any flow through the valve body.

Figure 2 shows a small MOV mounted on a butterfly valve whose disc is the moveable part that controls the flow through the valve body.

MOVs are typically controlled from a programmable logic controller (PLC) located near the valves, or from a central control room (Figure 3), which may consist of computers running sophisticated software, or an elaborate communications hub that is equipped with monitoring devices, diagnostic instruments, and communication software to link an entire system together. The operation of these valves is programmed based on the requirements of the application they are used for.

Automatically controlled MOVs are used when the valves are remote from the main work area, when the frequency of operation would require unreasonable human effort, or if rapid opening and closing of the valves is required. In some installations, the effort required to turn some large valves is beyond the capability of personnel. Some valves, if turned manually, require so much time and effort that the cycling operation would be self-defeating unless it was an emergency. Maintenance crews often carry portable valve actuators to work in outlying installations. On some valves, the drive motor and actuator can be disengaged so that a handwheel can be used to open or close the valve manually. In some installations, automatic and remote-control actuators are installed as a matter of convenience or to make possible a reduction of the number of employees required for the operation of a facility.

This module addresses various types of motor-operated valves, how they are set up, used, and repaired, and safety regulations that apply to MOVs.
