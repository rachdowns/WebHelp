3.2.1 Gas Actuators

A gas actuator is one of the most versatile units available. They are compatible with almost any kind of valve in any application. They are designed to operate off the pressure of the gas in the pipeline in which they are installed. They are designed to operate in a pressure range up to 1,500 psig. However, such units can be modified to use non-corrosive gases or hydraulic fluid from a central supply. Motors can be modified to operate off pressure as low as 15 psig.

CAUTION:

CAUTION

Users of these valves are cautioned to make certain the units are installed with accessories such as strainers and inline oilers on the supply line.

Gas actuators are installed with a supply line directly from the pipeline, with the strainer, lubricator, and line connected to the inlet port of a four-way shear-seal valve. Gas actuators operate on much the same principle as an old-fashioned waterwheel (Figure 10), and the motors used with pneumatic or hydraulic systems.

Sealed Valve

The Hansen sealed motor valve shown here is designed to eliminate valve stem leakage. The motor rotor is contained inside a sealed stainless steel can, which contains the fluid pressure. The motor stator is outside the can and is thus isolated from the fluid. One version of this valve has a built-in battery that provides power to close the valve in case of a power failure.

When the valve lever is moved automatically or manually, pressure is diverted to the opening or closing side to the motor inlet port. The direction the motor turns is determined by which port is opened. The opposite port exhausts the gas through another valve to the atmosphere. Valve travel is controlled by a limit switch. The limit switch mechanism causes the gas to be completely shut off (Figure 11).
