9.3.0 Modbus

Modbus is an open system communication protocol that is commonly used to communicate between two proprietary-protocol networks. The Modbus standard was developed by the Modicon Company in 1979, and it later became the trademark of Schneider Electric. Schneider Electric made Modbus available at no cost and gave the Modbus TCP/IP protocol specifications to the Internet Engineering Task Force. Modbus TCP/IP has become widely used and is popular in the industrial, metering, power generation, and electrical equipment industries.

The Modbus protocol incorporates both hardware and software standards. Modbus Remote Terminal Unit (Modbus RTU) communicates serially over RS-232 and RS-485 hardware connections. Modbus Transmission Control Protocol (Modbus TCP) communicates over Ethernet LAN-type hardware connections.

Modbus is the most common method for connecting industrial electronic equipment. It supports communication among a large number of devices connected to a network. Each device on the network is given a unique address. A command for a particular device contains its address. Although other devices may receive the command, only the intended device can act on it.

At the physical layer, the preferred cabling method uses the multipoint EIA-485 standard, which supports up to 32 devices over a common bus. The RS-232 standard is still in use. Either a two-wire or four-wire cabling configuration may be used. Baud rates range from 1.2kbps to 115kbps. However, all implementations must at least support 9.6kbps and 19.2kbps. The latter is the default rate.
