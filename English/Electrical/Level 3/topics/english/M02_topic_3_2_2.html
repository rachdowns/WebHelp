2.2.2 Resistance of Copper per Mil Foot

In many cases, it may be necessary to calculate the resistance of a certain length of wire or a busbar of a given size.

This can be done very easily if the unit resistance of copper is known. A unit called the mil foot may be used. A mil foot represents a piece of round wire that is 1 mil in diameter and 1' in length. This is a small enough unit to be very accurate for all practical calculations. A round wire of 1 mil in diameter has an area of 1 circular mil (CM), because the diameter multiplied by itself, or squared, is:

1 × 1 = 1   C M

The resistance of ordinary copper is 12.9Ω per mil foot at 75°C (167°F). This constant is important and should be remembered. It is normally represented by the letter K.

For example, determine the resistance of a piece of No. 12 copper wire that is 50' long. Remember that the resistance of any conductor increases as its length increases and decreases as its area increases. For a wire that is 50' long, first multiply 50' x 12.9Ω = 645Ω/mil ft. This is the resistance of a wire that is 1 CM in area and 50' long. NEC Chapter 9, Table 8 indicates that the area of a No. 12 wire is 6,530 CM, which will reduce the resistance in proportion. Now divide:

645 Ω ÷ 6,530   C M = 0.0988 Ω / m i l   f t

For another example, find the resistance of a coil containing 3,000' of No. 18 stranded wire. First multiply 3,000' × 12.9Ω = 38,700Ω/mil ft. Since the area of No. 18 wire is 1,620 CM, the total resistance may be found using the following equation:

38,700 Ω   m i l   f t ÷ 1,620   C M = 23.89 Ω  

NEC Chapter 9, Table 8 shows a resistance of 7.95Ω per 1,000' for No. 18 stranded AWG wire. For 3,000', use the following equation:

3 × 7.95 Ω = 23.85 Ω

The small difference between this answer and the answer in the first example is due to the use of approximate figures instead of lengthy decimal places.

The mil foot unit and its resistance of 12.9Ω for copper may also be used to calculate the resistance of square busbars by simply using the figure 0.7854 to convert from square mils to circular mils.

Suppose you wish to find the resistance of a square busbar that is 1⁄4" × 2" and 100' long. The dimensions in mils will be 250 × 2,000 = 500,000 square mils. To find the circular mil area, divide 500,000 by 0.7854 = 636,618.3 CM. Then multiply 100' × 12.9Ω = 1,290Ω, or the resistance of 100' of copper that is 1 mil in area. Because the area of this bar is 636,618.3 CM, divide:

1,290 Ω ÷ 636,618.3   C M = 0.002026 Ω   t o t a l   r e s i s t a n c e  

NEC Tables 310.15(B)(16) through (21) list the allowable current-carrying capacities of conductors with various types of insulation. These tables, however, do not take into consideration the length of the conductors or the voltage drop. Therefore, it is often necessary to use a larger size conductor than is indicated in the tables.
