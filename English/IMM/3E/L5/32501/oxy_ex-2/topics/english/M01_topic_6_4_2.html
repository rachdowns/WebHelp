5.4.2 Selective Catalytic Reduction (SCR)

Earlier sections describe catalytic actions, which are used to break down or convert some of the products that were initially separated at the atmospheric and vacuum distillation towers. A process capable of making similar conversions is a selective catalytic reduction (SCR) system. Figure 15 shows a typical SCR system.

Devices that burn fuels release nitrogen oxide and other pollutants in their exhausts. The furnaces of large boiler systems create large amounts of nitrogen oxide (NOx), which is a toxic gas that cannot safely be released into the atmosphere. When used with such furnaces, SCR systems are designed to convert nitrogen oxides into less dangerous diatomic nitrogen (N2) and water (H2O).

In an SCR system, ammonia, aqueous ammonia, or urea is mixed with the incoming flue gases and then passed through multi-layered beds of catalysts specifically designed to interact with the gases. The SCR system beds contain the catalysts used to clean the gases. The economizers shown in Figure 15 are the water tubes of a water-tube boiler. They are placed in the path of the flue gases to capture any remaining heat. By capturing the heat of the gases, they also help cool the gases before they are mixed with the ammonia solution.

Warning:

WARNING!

Pure anhydrous ammonia is dangerous to use and store. It is extremely toxic. Aqueous ammonia is safer than anhydrous ammonia, but still dangerous. Urea is the safest, but must be heated to be converted into ammonia.

Large induced-draft (ID) fans located near a facility’s smokestacks draw the flue gases from the furnace through the SCR system and a filtering system (usually a bag house) to the stacks. After the gases exit the SCR beds, they move down through an air preheater. The air preheater is a form of heat exchanger. On the other side of the preheater is another air flow moving in the opposite direction of the flue gases. That air is combustion air being force-fed into the furnace whose exhaust gases are being cleaned. Heat from the flue gases heats the combustion air going into the furnace.

From the preheater, the flue gases are sent out through one or more ID fans that send the gases on to another filtering system (such as a bag house) or to the stack for release into the atmosphere. If these ID fans do not work properly, or if they fail, the whole furnace and boiler system shuts down. Most companies use at least two such fans to reduce the chances of fan failure.

As mentioned earlier, SCR systems can also be used in a cracking process. Some materials other than the ammonia are mixed with the process gases, and some different catalysts may have to be used on the SCR beds, but the SCR process equipment could still be used in the same manner.
