6.2.1 Torquing and Tensioning Hardware

A company named HYTORC is one of several companies building, selling, and servicing such multifunctional hydraulic torquing and tensioning devices (see Figure 17). Rather than mix different makes of these tools in this manual, only the HYTORC brand of tools will be discussed. Other companies make similar tools, but for training purposes, this module will address only these tools.

Warning:

WARNING!

To prevent personal injury, only properly trained personnel should be allowed to operate these hydraulic torquing and tensioning tools.

The basic drive ratchet is a tool found in most maintenance technicians’ tool boxes. Torque wrenches (Figure 18) are usually specialty wrenches that should be kept in their own protective boxes. Torque wrenches are usually stored in a protected area within the maintenance area and only taken out when manual torquing is necessary.

Both wrenches have ratchet devices inside them that allow them to tighten (or loosen) bolts in one direction and then back up without disengaging from the bolt. A socket must also be used with each wrench to actually connect the drive portion of the wrench to a bolt. Without using some kind of cheater bar, the amount of twisting force applied to the bolt is limited to the strength of the person pulling on the handle of the wrench.

Another kind of ratcheting wrench has recently become available to maintenance personnel. It is a ratcheting box end wrench. These wrenches allow the user to place the box end of the wrench onto a bolt or nut and tighten or loosen in one direction and then back up without disengaging from the bolt or nut.

The reason these manual ratcheting wrenches are being discussed is because they are a manual version of the hydraulic torquing and tensioning tools shown earlier in Figure 17. The difference is that hydraulic fluid drives the gearing inside the drive portion of these tools. The head area of a basic hydraulic torque wrench is like the head area of the ratcheting box end wrenches. The hydraulic torquing wrenches are also equipped with safety handles that attach to the side of the wrench. Figure 19 shows the drive part of a hydraulic torque wrench.

A drive attachment made to fit into the drive head of the hydraulic torque wrench has a square drive end made to mate with the drive end of a socket. With the drive installed, the wrench is turned onto its side and the square drive end is pushed into a socket selected to fit the bolt to be torqued. Figure 20 shows a cutaway view of the hydraulic torque wrench and socket engaged with a bolt.

To tension a nut and bolt combination, a special washer or nut is used between the standard nut and the member being secured. Think of these devices as being special lock washers. Figure 21 shows some of these special tensioning nuts, often called discs.

Thousands of bolts and nuts are used in or on refinery machinery. Some vessels may have a hundred bolts and nuts securing only one cover. If each of those bolts and nuts does not properly do its job, there may be a leak, and a leak can lead to a fire, an explosion, and possible personal injury to nearby workers. Torquing and tensioning devices like the ones just mentioned are used to securely tighten all those bolts and nuts to their proper levels of tightness.

Also critical to a proper tightening process is the use of proper tightening techniques. The number of torquing and tensioning tools available will normally be limited by the maintenance budget. If only two tools are available and the job is to tighten the bolts in a set of flanges with eight bolts, the two bolts positioned 180 degrees from each other are tightened before the tools are moved on to the next set of bolts. The bolts are only tightened small amounts during each cycle of tightening so that the flanges are not warped. Figure 22 shows the recommended tightening patterns found on most vessels.

When multiple torquing and tensioning tools are used, hydraulic hoses are connected to form a daisy chain from one tool to the next with a hose going from the last tool back to the pump. All hydraulic hoses and tools are fitted with quick-disconnect connectors. Each tool has an input and an output connector. Some tools have multiple connectors. Figure 23 shows the hydraulic hose connectors normally found on these tools.

Bolts are usually only torqued part way during the first round of torquing. Consult the torquing specifications. When the tool is first placed onto the nut and activated, it will click. The clicking sound indicates that the hydraulic cylinder inside the tool has tightened the nut as far as it could with that stroke. Releasing the applied pressure will retract the cylinder and reset the tool for another try. Not all hydraulic tension wrenches can be set to stop at a particular pressure. With models that do not automatically stop, watch the pressure gauge to stop the increase. Extending and then retracting the cylinder one time is considered to be one cycle. During the final round of torquing, the cycles should be repeated until the tool stalls at the final preset torque/psi setting of the hydraulic system. To ensure that the tool has stalled, try one more cycle before removing the tool from the nut.
