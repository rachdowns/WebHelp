2.2.2 Separation (Fractionation)

When the heated crude oil is placed into an atmospheric tower, the different compounds inside the crude begin to reach their individual boiling points and flash into vapors. The vapors begin to rise through the tower, but the heaviest fractions remain at the bottom of the tower. When vapors condense, the liquid produced is called a distillate. Figure 4 shows where atmospheric distillation tower products or distillates are extracted.

As the flashed vapors rise inside the atmospheric distillation tower, they cool. The lighter fractions are extracted from the top of the tower while the heavier ones are extracted at lower elevations on the tower. All fractions extracted from the atmospheric distillation tower must be further processed in other refinery units. In most refinery operations, distillates are divided into three groups. The light distillates taken closer to the top of the tower become liquid petroleum gas (LPG), gasoline, and/or naphtha. Naphtha is a product that is highly volatile (unstable or explosive) and flammable. The middle distillates may become kerosene or diesel fuel. The heavier distillates taken toward the bottom of the tower may become home fuel oil, tar, wax, or lubricating oil or grease.

The following are descriptions of products commonly extracted at the atmospheric distillation tower:

  • Uncondensed gases – Any gases, often called off gases, are sent to a gas separation unit where any remaining light crude oil distillate is removed before the remaining gases are sent to the gas plant for recycling. LP gas is one product of such gases.

  • Light straight run (SR) naphtha – The next lightest product is the light straight run (SR) naphtha. Because of its nature, light SR naphtha is eventually used to make gasoline. Naphtha products are also used to make solvents because they can dissolve other products. After being extracted from the atmospheric distillation tower, SR naphtha is sent to a gasoline sweetening, treating, and blending process so that it can be blended to the desires of the end users.

  • Heavy SR naphtha – A heavier SR naphtha is extracted lower on the tower. It is sent to a hydrodesulfurization (HDS) treating unit where sulfur is removed from it. When sulfur burns, it produces sulfuric acid and pollutes the air. Removing sulfur from fuels makes them cleaner. The heavy SR naphtha is later processed into solvents, jet fuels, kerosene, and diesel fuel.

  • SR kerosene – SR kerosene is taken near the middle of the atmospheric distillation tower. The SR kerosene is sent directly to the distillate sweetening, treating, and blending process for final preparations.

  • SR middle distillate – The middle distillate is used in several other processes. It is sent to the following locations:

    – Directly to a sweetening, treating, and blending unit where it is prepared into a final product.

    – To a catalytic hydrocracking unit where hy­dro­gen is used to further break down its molecules. Hydrogen is also used to remove any remaining sulfur.

    – To a hydrodesulfurization (HDS) treating unit where sulfur is removed before it is further treated, sweetened, or blended into a final product.

    – To a catalytic cracking unit where it and other distillates are further broken down into various distillates used in a wide variety of products.

  • SR gas oil – Gas oil has a boiling range and viscosity that is a little higher than that of lubricating oil, but lower than that of kerosene. Gas oil from the atmospheric distillation tower is usually sent to a catalytic cracking unit to be further distilled.

  • Atmospheric tower residue – Any fraction that did not vaporize in the tower is considered to be residue. It is usually sent to a vacuum distillation tower where it will be distilled again, under vacuum, and at a higher temperature.

Extracting the raw product from the atmospheric distillation tower is only the first step in creating a finished product. Extracted products must be further processed in other more advanced processing equipment.
