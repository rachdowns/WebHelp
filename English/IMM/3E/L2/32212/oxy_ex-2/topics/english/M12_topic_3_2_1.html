2.2.1 Boiler Tubing

Boiler tubing is classified as steam-cooled or water-cooled. Steam-cooled tubing is found in the super-heater and reheat superheater. Both components have tubes that are susceptible to the effects of metal creep. Because most boilers selected for condition assessment are at least 20 years old, superheaters have become critical assessment components.

Creep is affected by temperature, stress, and operating time. The creep life of the superheater tubes is lowered by the higher operating temperatures, erosion, and corrosion that cause tube wall thinning and application of higher stresses. Excessive stresses from thermal expansion and mechanical loading can lead to tube cracking, regardless of the predicted creep life. Superheater tubing is susceptible to erosion, corrosion, or a combination of both.

Condition assessment of the superheater tubes includes visual inspection and tube sample analysis. Problems due to erosion, corrosion, expansion, or excessive temperature can generally be located by visual examination.

Examples of water-cooled tubing include economizer, boiler bank, and furnace tubing. The convection pass sidewall and screen tubes may also be water-cooled. Since these tubes operate at low temperatures, they are not as likely to suffer significant creep. Most boiler plants operate at high pressures. Since water-side deposits result in a loss of heat transfer capability (especially in the downcomer region), they must be chemically cleaned periodically. This will result in some tube material loss. Water chemistry control can help to prevent inside surface material loss that occurs as a result of operations and cleaning.

Downcomer tubing, in modern boiler designs, is becoming more susceptible to sediment buildup, erosion, and corrosion problems.

With the exception of creep deformation, the causes of reduced steam-cooled tubing life can also occur in water-cooled tubes. Erosion generally occurs on tube outside surfaces in the boiler or economizer bank from sootblowing or ash. Corrosion of water-cooled tubes is most likely in internal tube surfaces and comes from excessive water-side deposits. Deposit accumulations will promote corrosion.

The riser tubes are most commonly found in the penthouse or over the roof of the boiler. They carry the saturated steam-water mixture exiting the upper waterwall headers to the steam drum. The risers are not generally problem areas on boilers. Condition assessment includes ultrasonic measurements on nondrainable sections and on the outside of bends. When access is available, internal visual inspections with a fiberoptic or video probe should be done.

The supply tubes, normally found in the lower vestibule, ensure even distribution of the water from the downcomers to the furnace walls.

Header problems can be grouped together based on the header operating temperature. High-temperature steam-carrying headers are a major concern because they have a defined creep life and have high replacement costs. Lower temperature water and steam-cooled headers are not susceptible to creep, but may be damaged by corrosion, erosion, or severe thermal stress. The cold side tubing welds in headers are susceptible to cracking and corrosion.

The high-temperature headers, such as the superheater and reheater outlets that operate at temperatures of 900°F or higher, suffer creep under normal conditions. Figure 2 illustrates the locations where cracking is most likely to occur on high-temperature headers. High-temperature headers can also suffer from thermal and mechanical fatigue. Creep stresses together with thermal fatigue stress will lead to failure sooner than those resulting from creep alone.

The three factors affecting creep fatigue in superheater high-temperature headers are combustion, steam flow, and boiler load. Most boiler designs have burners arranged in the front and/or rear walls. This makes heat distribution inside the boiler not uniform.

Left-end burner inputs can vary, air distribution may not be uniform, and slagging and fouling can occur. The result of these combustion parameters is differences in heat input to individual superheater and reheater tubes. When combined with steam flow differences between tubes within a bank, the header experiences localized stresses much larger than those associated with steam pressure that can result in large ligament cracks (Figure 3).

Header expansion and contraction can result in fatigue cracks at support attachments, torque plates, and tube stub-to-header welds. Steam piping flexibility can cause header outlet nozzles to become over-loaded. These stresses result in externally initiated cracks at the outlet nozzle-to-header saddle weld.

Condition assessment of high-temperature headers should include a combination of NDE techniques targeted at the welds where cracks are most likely to develop. Creep of the header causes it to swell; the diameter should be measured at several locations on the header and the outlet nozzle. All major header welds, including the outlet nozzle, torque plates, support lugs, support plates, and circumferential girth welds should be examined. A percentage of the stub-to-header welds should also be examined. Header sections should be examined to locate the seam if it is not readily apparent. The seam weld is examined for surface imperfections by NDE.

The last test that should be performed on any high-temperature header (considered the most effective) is internal examination of at least two tube bore holes. These sites ideally are from the hottest location in the header.

The low-temperature headers operate at temperatures below which creep is a concern. These include waterwall headers, economizer inlet and outlet headers, and superheater inlet and intermediate headers. Damage to the low-temperature headers is generally caused by corrosion or erosion.

Waterwall headers are located outside the combustion zone. One exception is the economizer inlet header. This header may be in the gas stream and subjected to problems from temperature cycling. Boilers that are held for long periods in a hot standby condition without firing can experience severe damage to the economizer inlet header in a short time.

The size of the thermal shock is dependent on the difference between the unheated feedwater and the inlet header temperatures. The thermal shock is most severe near the header feedwater inlet and decreases as flow passes into the header and tubes. The main concern with other low-temperature headers is corrosion during downtime periods. Lower waterwall headers on stoker-fired boilers or those burning coal or refuse can suffer erosion along the sidewalls adjacent to the stoker grates.
