6.2.0 Guideline Procedures for Plugging Tubes in Drums and Headers

It is often permissible to plug the ends of an inaccessible failed tube and leave it in place. Tube replacement could require cutting out good tubes or baffles for access. This is seldom justifiable. When a tube is to be plugged, both ends must be plugged, and an opening must be cut in the tube to prevent pressure buildup. Waterwall tubes should be sectioned or replaced. Waterwall tubes or floor tubes should never be plugged in a recovery boiler.

Preferably, a rolled tube end leaking at the seat should be removed from its seat. The following options should be considered:

  • Roll in a new tube.

  • Roll in a new short stub section and plug it.

  • Seal weld the tube end to the drum shell.

  • If the drum shell is counterbored internally, a cylindrical plug must be installed and the plug seal welded to the drum.

Seal welding of plugs to the drum shell must be done with utmost caution, so as to minimize the heating of adjacent tube seats, which may cause loosening due to the heat generated during the welding process. Qualified welders should follow the proper welding procedures.

In most cases, removing the failed tube from its seat is not practical. In such a case, tapered plugs are used to plug the failed section (Figure 10).

Figure 10 also demonstrates the arrangement of the tapered plugs which are seal welded to the tube. Tapered plugs can only be used in drums or headers that are not internally counterbored.

The plugs must be tailor-made for each tube diameter and tube wall thickness. Figure 11 shows the de-tails of this tapered plug. This drawing is a common drawing for tube diameters from 13⁄4 inch OD through 41⁄2 inch OD and for any wall thickness.

For an internally counterbored tube hole, a cylindrical plug is used when the tube is still in the seat. Figure 12 and Figure 13 show the cylindrical plug for internally counterbored tube diameters of 13⁄4 inch OD through 21⁄2 inch OD, and 23⁄4 inch OD through 41⁄2 inch OD, respectively.

Some counterbores may be shallow enough that the tube ends are exposed sufficiently to permit seal welding to a tapered plug. If the tube seat is leaking, the tube must either be seal welded to the drum shell, or the counterbore is plugged with the cylindrical plug and sealed. In some cases, it may be necessary to machine the tube ends back in order to provide a seat for the cylindrical plug installation (Figure 14).

Economizer headers and superheater headers may be plugged as shown in Figure 15 and Figure 16, where external access is available and the plugs are not exposed to hot flue gases. When the headers are in the gas streams, a machined tube stub with a plug is used where the old tube can be removed from its seat with-out damage.

Tube stubs with external plugs that are below the horizontal centerline of the drum or header will not drain properly. This makes it necessary to remove the plug and clean the stub after chemical cleaning. The plug should be replaced. Care must be exercised in the plug removal process to not damage or thin the tube stub wall.

Machined tube stubs and plugs are used where the old tube can be removed from its seat without seat damage. This kind of construction is sometimes used when drum tube seat holes or header tube seat holes are provided for highly damaged drums and time prevents other repairs. The rolled end tube stub is extended into the shell, and a solid plug is installed and seal welded to the stub. These stubs and plugs are standardized to have only one tube stub and one plug for each standard tube hole and for all pressures up to and including 2,000 psi (Figure 17). This type of design is often seen in steam drums.

Before expanding the stubs in, they should be cleaned inside and out with a wire brush, abrasive paper, or liquid cleaner until the metal is clean of all foreign substances. In general, stubs do not require cleaning beyond the removal of dirt, rust, scale, or foreign material. The stub seat should be similarly cleaned.

The tube stub is properly expanded, and the wall thickness in the seat area is reduced by 6 to 10 per-cent for generating tubes, and 10 to 14 percent for other boiler tubes. The tube stub wall reduction for thin shells should be less than that for thicker shells to prevent over-expanding, which could cause adjacent tube seat leaks. Since the stub wall itself cannot be measured after it is rolled in its seat, the increase in the stub ID must be calculated to prove that the wall has, in fact, been reduced by the required percentages. This depends on the tube seat ID hole diameter, tube stub OD, and the clearance between the tube and the stub wall.

Plug all internal counterbored holes in the field with the cylindrical plug while the tube is still in the seat. Some counterbores may be shallow enough that the tube ends are exposed sufficiently to permit seal welding to a tapered plug. If the tube seat is leaking, the tube must either be seal welded to the drum shell or the counterbore plugged with the cylindrical plug and seal welded.
