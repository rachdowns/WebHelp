Section Two

2.0.0 PRECIPITATORS

When burned as a fuel source, all fossil fuels (except natural gas) create particulate or solid matter and ash in the flue gas. These particles must be removed to prevent pollution and the erosion of downstream components from the abrasive properties of these particles. Precipitators perform three basic functions. They remove the particles, keep the particulates from re-entering the flue gas, and discharge the particulates collected.

The most commonly used piece of equipment to accomplish all three of these functions is the electro-static precipitator (ESP). The ESP belongs to a group of equipment loosely called particle control equipment. The ESP is available in a dry (Figure 2) or wet (Figure 3) application. The ESP contains parallel plates through which the flue gas must travel. Between the plates there are electrodes that are charged using a very large power supply. When the flue gas passes through the field created, the particulates are attracted to the parallel plates (also called collection plates) and stick to them. Figure 4 demonstrates the collection principle.

The ESP has an efficiency of particulate removal higher than 97 percent. This far exceeds the removal efficiency of a fabric filter or baghouse.
