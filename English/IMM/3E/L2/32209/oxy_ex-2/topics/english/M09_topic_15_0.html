Section Fourteen

14.0.0 BLOWDOWN TANKS

Blowdown tanks are heavy-duty tanks that are designed to provide enough quench volume to condense the steam from boiler blowdowns when necessary. This is required when discharge of steam and hot water to the atmosphere and surrounding environment is not desired or allowed by local codes. Most local codes do not allow discharge of live steam due to the volatile chemicals used to maintain chemistry control in the boiler.

The steam is sent into the blowdown tank and is directed via a baffle down toward the water in the tank, which acts as a quench volume (Figure 36). As the steam is condensed, the water level in the tank rises. This causes an increase in the volume and, therefore, a change in the head pressure. The bottom of the tank is hooked to the outlet via a siphon break to the condensate system or the waste water system. If the waste water system is not rated for the discharge temperature, a cooler must be used.

The blowdown tank is vented at the top of atmosphere and is generally sized to be capable of receiving twice the normal amount of blowdown water and steam volume.

Maintenance on the blowdown tank generally includes inspection, replacing baffles due to erosion, corrosion repairs, and cleaning.

There are two types of blowdown: continuous or manual. Continuous blowdown has a tap in the boiler and a calibrated valve. This is optional in many boilers. There must be a means of manual blowdown on any boiler. It is necessary to blowdown a boiler at least once a shift, to keep the water column bowls clean.

The necessary steps to a proper manual blowdown are as follows:

Note:

NOTE

The boiler should be under a light load.

  1. Open the blowdown valve closest to the boiler.

  2. Crack the downstream valve slightly until the line warms up.

  3. Now open the valve until the water level in the sight valve drops about a half inch.

  4. Close the downstream valve quickly, being careful not to close it so tightly as to damage the valve packing.

  5. Close the first valve.

  6. If there is another set of blowdown valves, repeat the process. Be careful not to cause low water shutdown in the process.

Continuous blowdown systems frequently use a blowdown heat recovery system which can be used to warm up feedwater and save fuel. This also functions as a deaerator, reducing corrosion in the system by reducing oxygen and carbon dioxide content in the feedwater.

If the water becomes too low in the boiler, it may boil away completely, allowing the boiler to overheat. Boilers are provided with low-water shutoffs for the burners, to prevent damage to the equipment. It is also undesirable for the water to get too high, as the burners will be overworked supplying heat to the greater mass of cool water. Again, water shutoffs are installed with a level switch to control the amount of water in the boiler.
