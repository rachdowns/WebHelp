4.1.0 Steam Boilers

Combustion in a boiler occurs by combining fuel with oxygen and igniting the mixture. Methods used for combustion in gas and oil-fired boilers are basically the same as those used with furnaces. The ignition of the gas or oil can be achieved using a standing gas pilot, electric spark-type intermittent ignitor, or by direct electric ignition. The presence of a pilot or flame is proven by a protective device before the main gas or oil valve is allowed to open. Most boiler combustion systems have a purge and prepurge sequence to make sure that there is not a combustible mixture in the boiler which might cause an explosion during startup. The methods used to vent boilers are similar to those used with furnaces.

Steam boilers are divided into two broad categories: those that produce low-pressure steam and those that produce high-pressure steam. Low-pressure steam boilers have working pressures of up to 15 psi (250°F). High-pressure boilers operate at pressures from 15 psi and up. Low-pressure steam boilers are normally used in commercial applications, apartment houses, and single-unit industrial facilities. High-pressure boilers are used mainly to produce process steam for facilities such as a college campus, hospital, or industrial plant. The focus of this module is on low-pressure boilers used for comfort heating. The same kinds of boilers are used to produce steam as are used to produce hot water (Figure 5):

  • Cast iron boiler

  • Steel firetube and watertube boiler

  • Steel vertical tubeless boiler

  • Electric boiler

With the exceptions that follow, steam boilers are built and operate the same way as hot water boilers. Therefore, hot water boilers are not covered here.

  • Steam boilers are partially filled with water, while hot water boilers are entirely filled.

  • The pressure-temperature levels at which steam boilers operate are different.

  • The operating and safety controls used with steam boilers are different.
