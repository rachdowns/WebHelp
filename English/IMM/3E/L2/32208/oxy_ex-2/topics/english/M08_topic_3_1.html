2.1.0 Properties of Water

Depending on its heat content and temperature, water can exist in three states: ice (solid), water (liquid), and steam (water vapor or gas).

When water changes from one state to another, temperature changes can occur without registering on a thermometer. Figure 1 shows this graphically for water at sea level. If heat is added to one pound of ice at 0Â°F, a thermometer would show a rise in temperature until the reading reaches 32Â°F. This is the point at which the ice starts changing into water. If heat is continually added, the thermometer reading remains fixed at 32Â°F instead of rising. It continues to read 32Â°F until the entire pound of ice is melted. The increase in temperature from 0Â°F to 32Â°F registered by the thermometer is sensible heat. Sensible heat is heat that can be sensed by a thermometer or by touch. The heat that was added to the ice and caused its change in state from a solid to a liquid, but did not register on the thermometer is latent heat. Latent heat is the heat energy absorbed or rejected when a substance is changing states (solid to liquid, liquid to gas, or vice versa) without a change in the measured temperature.

If we continue to add heat to the pound of water, the thermometer will once again show an increase in temperature until the temperature reaches 212Â°F. At this point, the water starts boiling and changes state from water into steam (water vapor). As even more heat is added, the thermometer reading remains at 212Â°F until all the water has turned into steam. If we continue to add heat, the thermometer will once again register sensible heat.

As shown in Figure 1, it takes a great deal more heat to cause a change of state in water than is needed for a degree change in its temperature. It requires 144 Btus of latent heat to melt the pound of ice before the temperature began to rise. This is 144 times as much heat as is needed to raise the temperature of water one degree (specific heat). The change from water to steam required an even greater amount of latent heat. It took 970 Btus to change the water to steam. It is important to remember that none of this latent heat registered on the thermometer.

Figure 2 illustrates the common terms used in steam systems when defining the latent heat added or removed in changing water to and from the solid, liquid, or vapor states. It also shows the terms subcooling and superheat as they relate to sensible heat changes in water.

  • Latent heat of fusion is the heat that is gained or lost in changing ice to water or water to ice.

  • Latent heat of vaporization is heat that is gained in changing water to steam at a given pressure. The temperature at which this occurs is known as the boiling point or the saturation temperature. After all the water has been vaporized into pure steam at the saturation temperature, the steam is referred to as dry steam. It is also called saturated steam.

  • Latent heat of condensation is the heat given up or removed from steam in changing back to water.

  • Superheat is the measurable heat added to the steam after all the water has reached its boiling point and completely changed into steam. For example, the saturation temperature of water is 212Â°F. If the resulting steam is superheated 10Â°F, the steam temperature is 222Â°F. Steam that has been heated above the saturation temperature is called superheated steam.

  • Subcooling is the reverse of superheating. It is the temperature of the water after it has cooled below its condensing temperature. For example, the condensing temperature of water is 212Â°F. If the water is subcooled 10Â°F, the temperature is cooled to 202Â°F.

Figure 1 and Figure 2 show that most of the heat content of steam is stored as latent heat. It is this latent heat (970 Btu/lb at sea level) which makes steam so efficient as a carrier of heat. Large quantities can be transmitted efficiently with little change in temperature. This same amount of latent heat is released from the steam when it is condensed back into water. Steam is pressure and temperature dependent, which means that the system can be controlled by varying either the steam pressure or temperature.
