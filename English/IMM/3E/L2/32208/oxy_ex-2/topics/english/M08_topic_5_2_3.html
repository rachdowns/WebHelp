4.2.3 Low-Water Cutoff and Water Feeder Controls

Both low-water cutoff and water feeder controls are used to maintain safe water levels in a boiler.

Low-water cutoff controls (Figure 8) are used to protect boilers from damage caused by operation with too little or no water. There are several types of low-water cutoff controls. Many use a float that actuates a related electrical switch. The switch contacts are connected in the boiler’s burner control circuit. If the water in the boiler drops below the minimum safe level, the float drops and causes the switch contacts to open. This breaks the electrical circuit that controls the fuel supply applied to the boiler burners. As a result, the fuel supply to the burners is turned off. A marker on the low-water cutoff float bowl casting shows the cutoff level. The control is normally installed so that the marker on the float bowl is about 1⁄2-inch higher than the lowest visible point in the water gauge glass. The operating levels for the control are factory set and nonadjustable. Some low-water fuel cutoff controls have an opening through which a tool can be inserted and positioned to simulate a low-water condition.

Feeder controls maintain a constant water level in the boiler. There are many kinds of feeder controls. Some use a solenoid-operated valve controlled by the related low-water cutoff control. Some low-water cutoffs have a second switch that is actuated by the cutoff float. This switch is used to control the related solenoid-operated water valve (electric water feeder). This second switch operates at a higher water level than the cutoff level switch. If the boiler water drops to this first operating level, the float closes the switch, completing the electrical circuit to the water feeder. This energizes the water feeder solenoid-operated valve, allowing make-up water to flow through the valve and into the boiler. When the boiler water level is restored, the float level actuates the switch to open the electrical circuit to the water feeder. This shuts off the supply of make-up water to the boiler.

Controls are available that combine the low-water cutoff control with the water feeder control. These controls are called feeder-cutoff combination controls (Figure 9). Usually, the water feeder part of the control is mechanically actuated by the float. It is inactive as long as the condensate water returning to the boiler keeps the water level in the boiler at the normal operating level. If for some reason the boiler water drops below the normal operating level, the first thing the feeder cutoff combination control does is to add the amount of water required to keep the boiler in operation. It mechanically opens its feed valve, adds the small amount of make-up water necessary, and when the float reaches the normal water level, it closes again. A marker on the float bowl casting shows the feeder closing level. Feeders are normally installed so that the marker on the float bowl is about 2 to 21⁄2 inches below the normal water line.

If the feeder part of a combined feeder cutoff control cannot keep the water in the boiler at a safe level, the low-water fuel cutoff portion of the control will activate. When the water in the boiler drops below the minimum safe level, the float drops and opens the switch contacts, resulting in the fuel supply to the burners being turned off.

An electronic probe-type control is also commonly used as a low-water cutoff control. This device uses an insulated electrode whose tip is immersed in the water when it is at the normal operating level. Current flows from the electrode through the water to the housing of the probe (ground) to maintain a closed circuit. Should the water level drop below the normal safe level, the circuit to ground is broken. This prevents the firing of the boiler burners.
